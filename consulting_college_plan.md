# **2. What We Actually Found — The Real Patterns**

## **2.1 School Prestige Matters More in Consulting Than in Tech — But Non-Elites Still Exist**

The most consistent difference between this dataset and the SWE dataset: consulting skews significantly more toward elite schools. Wharton appears 3 times, Columbia twice, and Harvard, Yale, Brown, Georgetown, and Rice all appear in a sample of 20 profiles. That said, 4 of the 10 fully-read profiles attended non-elite undergraduate programs:

* Indiana University Kelley School of Business (Amala Neervannan → Bain)
* George Washington University School of Business (Lydia Melka-White → Bain)
* Boston University Questrom (Kelly Shao → BCG)
* Howard University — an HBCU (Oluchukwu Memeh → McKinsey)

In all four non-elite cases, the person had either (a) won a major case competition, (b) led a consulting-related club, (c) gone through a sophomore diversity program, or (d) done multiple internships before MBB. The non-elite path requires more deliberate effort to compensate for weaker recruiting pipelines.

| --- |
| **Key Insight**  The difference between consulting and tech: at Google and Meta, a Stony Brook student with 3 internships can compete equally with a Princeton student. In consulting, the school brand matters more — but non-elite is not disqualifying if you build the right signals. |

## **2.2 Majors Are Surprisingly Diverse — This Is NOT All Econ/Finance**

Across the 10 full profiles, the majors ranged far beyond what most people assume:

* Public Policy + Computer Science (Duke)
* Engineering (Howard)
* Computer Science + Management Science & Engineering (Stanford)
* Economics + Astrophysics (UChicago)
* BBA Marketing + Information Systems (Boston University)
* Industrial & Labor Relations (Cornell ILR)
* Business (Indiana / GWU)
* MBA + MEd (UVA Darden — post-MBA hire)

The common thread is not the major — it's quantitative aptitude (ability to work with numbers and data) plus structured thinking (ability to break down a problem). Business and economics majors are common, but so are engineering, CS, and even labor relations. No one in this sample had a purely arts or humanities major at the undergrad level, though Lydia (GWU Business) had a heavy arts co-curricular background.

## **2.3 The ACI/Summer Associate Internship Is THE Gateway**

Every undergrad consultant in this sample — without exception — got their full-time offer by returning from an internship:

* Bain's Associate Consultant Intern (ACI) program → return offer → full-time ACI
* BCG's Summer Associate role → return offer → full-time Associate
* McKinsey's Business Analyst/Consultant Analyst internship → return offer → full-time

This makes the junior-year internship in consulting even more critical than in tech. In tech, some people got their first FAANG offer from cold applications. In consulting, every path in this sample was: internship → return offer → done. Nobody cold-applied to full-time consulting roles after graduating.

|  |
| --- |
| **Critical Timeline**  MBB recruiting is compressed and early. McKinsey, BCG, and Bain typically open recruiting for summer internships in August–October of your junior year. If you're not prepared by the start of your junior year, you've already missed the window. Case prep has to begin sophomore year. |

## **2.4 Sophomore Diversity Programs Are the Real Entry Point**

Just like STEP/New Technologist in tech, MBB each run programs specifically for freshmen and sophomores from underrepresented backgrounds. Lydia Melka-White explicitly named Deloitte's early diversity program as her entry into professional consulting, which led to her Bain offer two years later. She also mentioned Bain Emerging Leaders (BEL) as open to sophomores.

* Bain Emerging Leaders (BEL) — for underrepresented sophomores; applications open ~Feb 1
* McKinsey Freshman Summit / Early Insight (varies by year)
* BCG Consulting Challenge / Early Access Programs
* Deloitte Discovery Intern Program — Lydia's specific entry point
* Oliver Wyman's Black Leaders in Business / Women in Business programs

These programs are the consulting version of Google STEP. They give you access to mentors, case workshops, and a head start on networking before you ever apply to the full internship. Three of the four non-elite school consultants in this dataset explicitly used diversity programs as their on-ramp.

## **2.5 Case Competitions Are the Single Highest-Leverage Activity**

In tech, the equivalent signal is building a project or getting a research publication. In consulting, it's winning a case competition. Amala Neervannan (Indiana → Bain) won two separate case competitions — the National Diversity Case Competition and the International Global Scaling Challenge — before she had a single MBB offer. This was her primary differentiator as a non-target school student.

* National Diversity Case Competition (hosted by Kelley School of Business, Indiana) — open to students from all schools
* International Global Scaling Challenge
* Deloitte Case Competition
* Darden Case Competition (UVA)
* MIT Sloan Case Competition

Case competitions achieve two things simultaneously: they develop the structured problem-solving skills tested in case interviews, and they produce a visible, verifiable credential that differentiates you from other applicants. Lydia also directly described her case prep process: 6 weeks, Management Consulted platform, watching video examples, and practicing with partners.

## **2.6 Club Leadership Follows a Very Specific Pattern**

Across all 10 profiles, leadership in specific types of organizations appears repeatedly:

* Consulting clubs: BU Consulting Group President (Kelly), Stanford Consulting Club (Alahji), Duke Impact Investing Group (Shrey, Emilio)
* Student government: Duke Student Government (Shrey, major involvement), Darden Student Association President (Tyler)
* Identity/community organizations: NSBE (Alahji), Black Business Student Association (Tyler), Engineers Without Borders President (Oluchukwu)
* Founding something: GW Gospel Choir (Lydia), FIRST @ Duke (Shrey)
* Fellowship programs: Robertson Scholars (Emilio), Aga Khan Foundation Fellowship (Emilio), Gates Scholarship (Alahji)

Teaching and TAing also appeared multiple times: Kelly Shao TAed 4 different courses at BU. This is identical to the SWE pattern and suggests that TA experience is broadly valued across both paths.

## **2.7 International and Social-Sector Experience Is More Valued in Consulting Than Tech**

Three of the six McKinsey consultants had significant international or nonprofit experience before their offer:

* Emilio Abelmann: Aga Khan Foundation Fellow (Kyrgyzstan/Geneva) + consulting work in Kenya before McKinsey
* Shrey Majmudar: White House NSC (geopolitics/APEC), FBI Counterintelligence — national security focus
* Bella Vandenberg: Cornell Vietnam Engaged Learning Program (labor research on manufacturing + pregnancy discrimination) — led directly to her honors thesis and differentiated her Bain application

This pattern is distinct from the SWE world, where international work rarely appeared. Consulting firms, especially McKinsey, value breadth of experience and exposure to diverse contexts. The case for hiring someone who has worked in Kyrgyzstan, studied labor conditions in Vietnam, or worked on US geopolitics is stronger in consulting than in software.

## **2.8 Non-Traditional Paths Exist — But They're Still Structured**

Two profiles stand out as genuinely non-traditional:

* Shrey Majmudar: FBI Counterintelligence → White House NSC → McKinsey. He never did a finance or consulting internship. His entry was national security credibility and a Duke education with massive student government involvement.
* Tyler Kelley: Worked as Director of Recruitment at Indiana University's International Studies school for two years, then went back for an MBA (Consortium Fellow at UVA Darden), did a Deloitte internship, then joined McKinsey as an Associate Consultant. The entire timeline from undergrad to McKinsey was 6+ years.

Both non-traditional paths share something: each step was deliberate and built a specific credential. The FBI→White House→McKinsey path isn't random — it's a coherent national security expertise story. The higher-ed → Darden MBA → McKinsey path is the classic 'post-MBA hire' track.

# **4. The Consulting Roadmap — Built From Real Profiles**

This section is grounded in what actually appears across these 10 profiles — not generic consulting advice.

## **Phase 1: High School**

**What Appeared on These Profiles**

* Academic excellence in a broad range of subjects: Student Marshal (top 1% of UChicago), Summa Cum Laude, Phi Beta Kappa, National Merit
* Structured problem-solving from early on: Chess ELO 2307 (Henry), FIRST Robotics (Shrey), pre-college programs (Yale Global Scholar)
* National Merit Hispanic Scholar (Emilio) — competitive scholarship recognition is visible to recruiting
* Prep schools: Phillips Exeter Academy (Emilio). Not universal, but a consistent signal in consulting more than tech
* Building or leading something: Shrey founded a FIRST Robotics team at his high school, led NHS, served on Student Council

**What Does NOT Appear**

* Pure academic credentials alone — everyone in this sample had extracurriculars that showed leadership or breadth
* Single-dimensional profiles: the chess player was also a Student Marshal; the robotics founder was also in student government

## **Phase 2: College — Year 1 & 2 (The Setup Phase)**

**Apply to Sophomore Diversity Programs Early**

This is the highest-leverage action a freshman or sophomore can take in consulting. Applications open in the fall of your freshman or sophomore year:

* Bain Emerging Leaders (BEL) — sophomores; applications ~February 1
* McKinsey Early Insight / Freshman Summit — freshmen and sophomores from underrepresented groups
* BCG Early Access / Exploring BCG programs — varies; check bcg.com/careers
* Deloitte Discovery Intern — early career, underrepresented students (Lydia's path)
* Oliver Wyman's Black Leaders in Business / Women in Business

**Join — and Lead — a Consulting Club**

The consulting club pattern is near-universal. Kelly Shao spent 3 years in BU Consulting Group, starting as a StrategyLab analyst and ending as President. Shrey and Emilio both listed Duke Impact Investing Group. Alahji listed Stanford Consulting Club. Do not just join — progress toward leadership.

**Start Casing Sophomore Year**

Lydia's timeline: Deloitte early diversity internship (sophomore, no casing required) → realized she needed to re-recruit → 6 weeks of intensive casing → Bain offer. That 6 weeks was possible only because she had some foundation. Starting case prep cold in January of junior year is too late for most people.

* Platform: Management Consulted (filter by firm and round; free Wharton/Ross casebooks exist)
* Method: Watch consultants case on video first → then practice with a partner → then do timed solo practice
* Find a casing partner: Lydia met hers at a company exploration weekender. The relationship is worth it beyond the prep.

## **Phase 3: Junior Year — The Make-or-Break Summer**

**Applications Open August–October. This Is Not a Joke.**

MBB recruiting is notoriously early. McKinsey, BCG, and Bain begin accepting applications for the following summer's internship in August and September of your junior year — in some cases before junior year even starts. If you haven't done case prep, attended networking events, and gotten your resume reviewed by October of junior year, you are behind.

**Case Interview Structure**

* Round 1: Typically 2 case interviews. PEI (Personal Experience Interview) + case. Focus on structured problem decomposition.
* Round 2 (Final): 3–4 case interviews over one day. Interviews with partners and senior leaders.
* Practice target: 50+ cases minimum, 100+ for competitive candidates. Quality over quantity — get real feedback.

**Networking Is Not Optional**

Multiple profiles show 'thank you' posts naming specific Bain/McKinsey employees who helped them through recruiting. This is not courtesy — it's evidence that the network was activated. Attend office events, company exploration weekenders (Lydia mentioned one explicitly), and information sessions. Build relationships before you apply.

| --- |
| **From Lydia's Post**  'My friend Zoe and I (who met during a company exploration weekender in Chicago) were constantly casing together.' — The networking event produced both a casing partner and a peer support relationship. |

## **Phase 4: Senior Year and First Job**

* If you got a return offer: evaluate which practice area and office aligns with your long-term goals. Office matters — McKinsey NYC vs. SF vs. DC have different project profiles.
* If you didn't get a return offer: Tier 2 consulting (Deloitte, Accenture, Oliver Wyman, L.E.K., A.T. Kearney) → MBB lateral is a real path. Tyler went Deloitte → McKinsey.
* MBA as path corrector: If undergrad recruiting didn't go to MBB, a top MBA (HBS, Wharton, Booth, Kellogg, Columbia) resets your access entirely. The Consortium Fellowship (Tyler's path) is specifically designed for URMs pursuing MBA → consulting.

# **5. Consulting vs. SWE — What's Actually Different**

| **Dimension** | **Software Engineering** | **Management Consulting** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| School prestige | Matters less — strong internship > elite school | Matters more — target school list is narrower |
| Major | Must be CS/Engineering/STEM | Can be anything — econ, policy, ILR, business, CS |
| Key skill test | Technical coding interview (LeetCode) | Case interview (structured problem-solving + math) |
| Key club | CS club, research lab, hackathon team | Consulting club, case competition, investment club |
| Sophomore program | Google STEP, Microsoft New Technologist | Bain Emerging Leaders, McKinsey Early Insight |
| Path to FT offer | Internship → return offer (very common) | Internship → return offer (nearly universal) |
| International experience | Rarely appeared in profiles | Very common; often a differentiator |
| Non-linear paths | Common (Capital One → Twitter → Meta) | Exist but less common at undergrad level; MBA is the main non-linear path |
| Timeline pressure | Applications open Aug–Dec of junior year | Applications often open Aug–Oct — even earlier |
