# Tsadiku Obolu — Voice & Writing Style Guide

Use this skill before writing any site content for Obolu Blueprint. The goal is to write at a level where someone reading it could identify it as Tsadiku's — not a website, not a brand, not a copywriter. Him.

The primary source for this skill is his UC Application PIQs (2019). The TikToks inform his perspective and what he cares about, but his written voice is what this skill is built around.

## His written voice — studied from the PIQs

### Vocabulary

Tsadiku's word choices are precise without being showy. He uses words that are specific and earned — not to impress, but because they're the right word. He has an academic vocabulary that he uses naturally, not performatively.

Words and phrases he actually uses:
- **"incessant"** — not "constant" or "nonstop"
- **"ineffectual"** — not "ineffective" or "not working"
- **"actualize"** — *"gave them an opportunity to actualize academic futures"*
- **"lingua franca"** — he uses domain-specific terms when they fit
- **"pedagogy"** — he learned the word, it's now his
- **"insatiable"** — *"my insatiable love for math"*
- **"congruent, consistent, and even harmonious"** — mathematical precision applied to values
- **"catalyst"** — *"these observations served as an important catalyst"*
- **"institutionalized racism"** / **"institutionalize change"** — he uses the same root word in two directions deliberately
- **"interdisciplinary"** — he thinks across fields and says so
- **"restorative justice"** — policy vocabulary used naturally
- **"culturally competent"** — *"culturally competent math strategies"*
- **"scholastic recognition"** — formal but not stiff
- **"preventable"** — *"untimely, preventable deaths"* — he adds the moral weight with one word

### Sentence structure

**He opens with a scene or a person, never a thesis.**
> *"I was the same age as Tamir Rice when he was killed in Ohio."*
> *"If I could have any superpower, it would be to grab the sun and bring it towards the earth to melt all the guns."*

He drops you into something specific before you know where it's going. The argument comes later.

**He builds using formal connectives — not casual transitions.**
- "Unfortunately" / "However" / "Soon thereafter" / "Subsequently"
- Not "but" at the start of a sentence. Not "also." He uses connectives that show he's moving deliberately.

**He uses parallel structure for weight.**
> *"From Mike Brown to Philando Castille, from Oscar Grant to Trayvon Martin"*
> *"We learned together, chanted, and developed a strategy"*
> *"congruent, consistent, and even harmonious"*

Three-part constructions appear often. He builds rhythm through repetition of form.

**He shows agency through contrast.**
> *"Instead of quitting the organization, in the 10th grade I ran for BSU President."*
> *"While school was a place for my empowerment, this was not the norm."*

The structure "Instead of X, I Y" or "While X, Y" shows character through choice — not by saying "I'm a leader" but by showing what he did when the easier path was available.

**He lets accomplishments land without commentary.**
> *"Within a short period of time, I managed to transform our student club. We grew quickly from 3 members to 150."*
> *"I was selected for the Summer Math And Science Honors (SMASH) program at UC Davis."*

He states what happened. He doesn't add "which was a huge achievement" or "I was proud of this." The fact speaks.

**He ends forward — toward the future, not backward at what he did.**
> *"In college, I want to apply an interdisciplinary approach to address global challenges like gun violence."*
> *"I am confident that even the hardest equations of injustice can be solved through mathematical symmetry."*

Paragraphs and essays close by pointing somewhere. Not summarizing. Moving.

### Rhetorical moves

**He makes the local global.**
- The BSU at one high school in Sacramento → national police brutality
- A math teacher's classroom method → engineering social justice
- A chip business → leadership development
- A shooting at a party → gun violence as a systemic problem

He never lets a personal story stay only personal. It always connects outward.

**He holds tension without resolving it cheaply.**
> *"I saddens me to admit that I didn't tell anyone. At the time, I didn't know what to do. Violence had already become such a common experience for me and my peers, that we just carried on like nothing had happened."*

He doesn't rush to the lesson. He sits in the honest uncomfortable moment first.

**He uses precision to convey scale.**
> *"I have worked with dozens of middle and high school students."*
> *"I have trained 38 juniors and seniors"*
> *"Over 400 Inderkum High School students from all racial and ethnic backgrounds participated"*

Specific numbers. Not "many" or "hundreds." The specificity signals he was actually there and counted.

**He uses metaphor that comes from his subject matter.**
> *"In a world of inequities, numbers are congruent, consistent, and even harmonious."* — math as equity
> *"The bullet didn't just hit him, it ricocheted throughout our lives."* — physics of violence
> *"The hardest equations of injustice can be solved through mathematical symmetry."* — engineering as justice

His metaphors come from math, engineering, and physics — the fields he studied. They're not decorative. They're how he actually thinks.

## How he talks to students (from TikTok — tone reference, not word-for-word)

The TikToks show how Tsadiku connects with his actual audience: Black high school students, many of whom have been told college isn't for them. This isn't language to copy onto the site — it's the energy and logic to carry into whatever you write.

**He names the objection first, then answers it.**
Every TikTok starts from something a skeptic in the audience actually believes — "school is boring," "nothing is guaranteed," "my cousin went to college and doesn't have a job." He takes it seriously before he takes it apart. Website content should do the same: open with what the reader is already thinking, then give them the real answer.

**He uses math and data, but walks through it like a conversation.**
He doesn't drop a statistic. He builds it step by step — here's what you'd make if you didn't go, here's what you'd spend, here's what's left. By the end the reader did the math with him. That approach translates directly to the site.

**He speaks to the reader like they're capable.**
He never condescends. The premise is always: you're smart, you just didn't have the information. That assumption should be underneath every sentence on this site.

**He challenges without lecturing.**
He'll say "I'm sick of Black kids being pushed toward routes with a 0.001% success rate" — but it doesn't feel preachy because he immediately pivots to: here's the better route, here's why, here's how. The feeling is frustration on your behalf, not disappointment in you.

**He keeps the long game in view.**
He talks about living "a good 80 to 100" — not just getting money, but having a life. Travel, experiences, being able to turn up when you want to. That framing — college as access to a full life, not just a career — should run through the site.

**What this means for the website:**
- Keep the directness. Short sentences when making a point.
- Keep the honesty. Say what's real, including uncomfortable things.
- Keep the data. Numbers matter to him and they persuade his audience.
- Don't water down the stakes. These are real decisions with real consequences.
- Ditch the slang. "Tap in," "get put on," "bro" — those work on TikTok. On the site, carry the same energy in cleaner language.

## A note on vocabulary and audience

The site is for high school students — many of them first-generation, many from low-income communities. Words like "pedagogy," "lingua franca," or "culturally competent" are from Tsadiku's PIQs written for college admissions officers. They're not wrong — they show his range — but they're not the right register for this audience.

The target is: precise without being academic. Clear without being dumbed-down. The reader should feel like they're getting real information from someone who's been where they are and figured it out — not reading a college prep pamphlet.

When in doubt: would a sharp 16-year-old in Sacramento read this sentence and immediately understand it? If yes, keep it. If no, rewrite it.

## The throughline

Whether writing or speaking, Tsadiku does three things consistently:

1. **Grounds it in something real and specific** — a name, a number, a moment you can picture
2. **Connects that specific thing to a system or pattern** — this isn't just my story, this is how it works
3. **Points toward action or the future** — what he did, what you can do, where this goes

## What NOT to do

- ❌ Don't use slang or TikTok language on the website — that's a different medium and a different context
- ❌ Don't hedge: "it might be worth considering" / "students may find it helpful to" — say the thing directly
- ❌ Don't use vague inspirational filler: "believe in yourself," "stay focused," "you've got this"
- ❌ Don't summarize at the end of a section — close by pointing forward
- ❌ Don't self-congratulate: never write "this was an incredible achievement" or "remarkably" — let the fact stand alone
- ❌ Don't use casual connectives ("also," "plus," "and then") in formal writing — use "subsequently," "moreover," "however"
- ❌ Don't write generic — if you can't name the specific person, school, number, or moment, you're not specific enough yet
- ❌ Don't make the metaphor arbitrary — his metaphors come from math and engineering because that's his lens. Keep metaphors tied to something real in his world

## Sentence patterns to use

- Open with a specific person or moment, then expand outward
- "Instead of [expected response], I [actual response]." — character through choice
- "From X to Y, from A to B" — parallel pairs that build weight
- "[Specific number] + [specific group] + [specific action]" — "I trained 38 juniors and seniors in culturally competent math strategies"
- "While [X was true], [Y was also true]." — holding two things at once
- "In [future context], I want to [specific goal]." — close by pointing forward
- State the accomplishment. Do not explain why it was impressive.
