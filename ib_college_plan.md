## **SECTION 2 — REAL PATTERNS**

**Pattern 1: IB is more school-dependent than consulting**

Every single person in our dataset attended a recognizable university with a strong finance reputation — whether that's Harvard, Wharton, Cornell, or Berkeley Haas. Unlike tech or even consulting, there were almost no truly off-the-radar schools. The one exception was Iruo James from University of Georgia (Terry College) at JPMorgan — but even then, Terry is a ranked business school with a known IB recruiting presence.

|  |
| --- |
| **Key Finding**  IB recruits heavily on-campus at a short list of 'target' schools. If your school isn't on a bank's official recruiting list, getting in requires extraordinary effort — diversity programs, lateral moves, or an MBA later. |

**Pattern 2: Diversity Programs Are Real Pipelines**

Two people in our dataset explicitly got into JPMorgan through the Advancing Black Pathways Fellowship Program (ABP): Brandon Robinson (Michigan Ross) and Rhema Onyia (Cornell). Both announced it publicly on LinkedIn. This is not a token program — it routes participants directly into the Corporate & Investment Banking Division.

Goldman Sachs runs a parallel program: the Virtual Insight Series. Rhema Onyia was selected for it out of 10,000+ applicants as a first step in the GS recruiting pipeline. These programs exist across every bulge bracket bank and serve as a structured entry point for underrepresented students at both target and semi-target schools.

|  |
| --- |
| **Key Programs to Know**  JPMorgan: Advancing Black Pathways (ABP), Launching Leaders. Goldman Sachs: Virtual Insight Series, Possibilities Summit, Freshman/Sophomore Programs. Morgan Stanley: Sophomore IB Diversity Program. Citi: Freshman Discovery Program. These are competitive but accessible before junior year. |

**Pattern 3: IB Has Verticals — And They Matter**

IB is not one job. The people we found were working in specific coverage groups: TMT (Technology, Media & Telecom) at Goldman Sachs, ECM Healthcare (Equity Capital Markets) at JPMorgan, LATAM (Latin America) at JPMorgan, FIG (Financial Institutions Group) at Morgan Stanley, and Technology IB at Morgan Stanley. Knowing which vertical you're targeting shapes your recruiting strategy.

Mateo Graziosi (NYU Stern) landed on JPMorgan's LATAM team — his Spanish surname and international background likely contributed. Fiona Popescu (USC) landed on the ECM Healthcare team. Specialization opens doors that general 'I want to do IB' pitches don't.

**Pattern 4: Pre-IB Internships Are a Real Route**

Zoe Zhao (Georgetown Walsh) didn't go straight to JPMorgan. She first landed a summer internship at UBS Investment Banking working on Technology and Media deals in New York. From there, she moved to JPMorgan full-time. In her own words: 'Two years ago today, I would have never thought I could go into Investment Banking.'

This 'boutique or mid-tier bank first, bulge bracket second' route is common in IB. It's harder to get the first IB internship than you think — but getting ANY investment banking internship opens the door to the top banks later.

|  |
| --- |
| **The IB Internship Ladder**  Sophomore: Corporate finance or boutique bank internship → Junior: Bulge bracket summer analyst (this is the recruiting conversion moment) → Senior: Full-time offer. Most FT offers come from the junior-year summer. You must convert your summer analyst role. |

**Pattern 5: The Alumni Network Is Explicitly Cited**

Zoe Zhao wrote publicly that she is 'deeply grateful for Georgetown alums, mentors and friends' who supported her throughout recruiting. This wasn't vague — she was specifically thanking the Georgetown alumni network for helping her break in. IB recruiting is heavily relationship-driven and alumni who are already at the banks will pull you into interviews if they know you.

Rhema Onyia (Cornell) also helped a peer get an internship at Goldman Sachs — demonstrating that once you're in the network, you're expected to help pull others in. IB is tribal, and your school's alumni presence at a specific bank is often the deciding factor in whether you get a first-round interview.

**Pattern 6: UC System Is the West Coast Feeder — Not Just for Tech**

Three Morgan Stanley analysts in our dataset attended UC schools: Nathan Vuong (Berkeley Haas), Tobin Thannickal (Berkeley Haas, Tech IB), and Celina Pan (UC Berkeley). Shriya Jain (UCLA) is at Morgan Stanley as well. The California UC schools — especially Berkeley Haas and UCLA Anderson — are legitimate IB feeder schools for the San Francisco, LA, and even New York offices of bulge brackets.

## **SECTION 4 — THE IB ROADMAP**

**Freshman Year**

* Start with a Finance or Business major — Economics also works at non-business schools. Accounting or Financial Engineering are strong signals.
* Join your school's Investment Banking Club, Finance Club, or equivalent. These clubs run mock interviews and have alumni relationships with banks.
* Apply to Freshman/Sophomore diversity programs: Goldman Sachs Possibilities Summit, JPMorgan Launching Leaders, Morgan Stanley Sophomore IB Program. Applications open in September–November.
* Learn Excel and PowerPoint at a high level. IB analysts build financial models and pitch decks — the earlier you can do this, the better.
* Get your GPA above 3.7 and keep it there. IB has GPA screens that consulting and tech do not.

**Sophomore Year — The Most Important Year in IB**

* Apply to every sophomore diversity and early insight program you qualify for. These programs often lead directly to junior-year internship offers.
* Pursue any finance internship you can get — wealth management, corporate finance, boutique bank, financial services firm. Any finance experience beats no experience.
* Begin building your network with alumni at target banks. LinkedIn cold-outreach to alumni for 15-minute informational calls. Ask about their group, their path, and what they look for in candidates.
* Study the structure of IB: coverage groups (TMT, Healthcare, FIG, Energy, Real Estate, LATAM), product groups (M&A, ECM, DCM, Leveraged Finance). Know which group you want and why.
* Junior year recruiting starts in September of junior year — meaning your preparation must begin in the spring of sophomore year. This is not an exaggeration.

**Junior Year — This Is The Moment**

* IB summer analyst recruiting begins in August/September of junior year for the FOLLOWING summer. Banks recruit an entire year in advance.
* Submit applications to bulge bracket banks (Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Citi, Barclays, Deutsche Bank) and elite boutiques (Lazard, Evercore, Centerview, PJT, Moelis) simultaneously.
* The junior-year summer internship is the conversion moment — most analysts receive full-time offers from their summer internship. If you don't get a return offer, you re-recruit senior year.
* Prepare for technical interviews: LBO models, DCF analysis, comparable company analysis, precedent transactions, accounting questions. Use resources like Breaking Into Wall Street (BIWS) or Wall Street Prep.
* Prepare for behavioral interviews: Why IB? Why this bank? Walk me through a deal. Why this group? Every answer must be specific and banker-like.

**Senior Year**

* If you received a return offer from your junior summer: you're done. Accept it and start senior year in peace.
* If you did NOT convert your summer offer: recruit immediately in September–October for any remaining open analyst roles or boutique positions.
* Consider a pre-MBA IB role: some people enter corporate finance or financial advisory and move to IB at 24–26 after demonstrating finance skills.
* Analyst programs typically last 2–3 years, after which analysts go to private equity, hedge funds, corporate development, or MBA programs (the classic exit path).

## **CLOSING NOTE**

Investment banking is the most school-dependent of the three careers in the Obolu Blueprint. The school you attend will determine which banks recruit on your campus, which alumni you have access to, and how hard you have to work to get your first interview. That said, the data shows clear pathways from semi-target schools like USC, Georgetown, and NYU Stern — but they all required: (1) knowing which vertical to target, (2) doing a boutique or smaller firm internship first, or (3) using a formal diversity fellowship program as the entry point. The students who break in from non-target schools are the ones who run the full playbook, not just part of it.

| **The IB Formula**  Target or semi-target school + 3.7+ GPA + IB club leadership + diversity program application + alumni network outreach + boutique internship (if needed) + junior-year summer analyst conversion = Investment Banking Analyst at a bulge bracket. |
